Final Atlas 5 Launches for Amazon LEO
A United Launch Alliance Atlas V 551 rocket lifted off from Space Launch Complex 41 at Cape Canaveral Space Force Station at 12:30 a.m. Eastern on July 2, carrying 29 Amazon Leo satellites -- designated mission LA-07 -- in the final Atlas V flight to carry a satellite payload. This was the last of nine Atlas V missions Amazon contracted with ULA in 2021; the constellation now has more than 390 satellites in orbit and future Amazon Leo launches will transition to ULA Vulcan, Ariane 6, New Glenn, and Falcon 9 vehicles. (Source: SpaceNews, July 2, 2026)
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
The retirement of the Atlas 5 as an Amazon LEO delivery vehicle concentrates future constellation build-out on a shorter list of launch providers, raising competitive and schedule risk for Amazon LEO as it races Starlink for broadband market share.

BREAKING North America CRITICAL · 88
Hegseth Launches Centralised Pentagon Drone Office
Defence Secretary Hegseth signed a memo dated June 29 establishing a new Direct Reporting Portfolio Manager for Unmanned Systems (DRPM-UxS), announced publicly on July 1. The office consolidates oversight of almost all Pentagon drone and autonomous systems programmes -- including all UAS Groups 1-3, autonomous ground vehicles, and most unmanned surface vessels -- but excludes major unmanned airframes such as the Collaborative Combat Aircraft and the Navy Medium Unmanned Surface Vessel. The DRPM-UxS will report to Deputy Defence Secretary Stephen Feinberg; no interim director had been named as of July 2. (Sources: Breaking Defense, DefenseScoop, USNI News, July 1, 2026)
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
Centralising drone authority at the Pentagon level will accelerate procurement cycles and concentrate contract awards, creating a clearer and higher-volume pipeline for drone and counter-drone vendors including radar makers, EW integrators, and autonomous systems manufacturers.

BREAKING Europe CRITICAL · 85
Lithuania Moves to End Nuclear Weapon Ban
As of July 2, Lithuania parliamentary party leaders reached broad political consensus to remove -- not merely amend -- Article 137 of the constitution, which bans weapons of mass destruction and foreign military bases from Lithuanian territory. President Gitanas Nauseda confirmed the agreement after meeting with parliamentary faction leaders on July 2, describing the provision as outdated. The change requires two-thirds majorities in two separate parliamentary votes; Parliament Speaker Juozas Olekas said adoption could occur by end of 2026. No immediate plans to store nuclear weapons in Lithuania were announced. (Sources: Reuters via KFGO, LRT, CNBC, July 2, 2026)
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
If Lithuania proceeds, it will mark the first formal nuclear posture shift by a NATO frontline state in this conflict cycle, with material implications for alliance burden-sharing, US extended deterrence commitments, and demand for nuclear-capable delivery platforms from European primes.
