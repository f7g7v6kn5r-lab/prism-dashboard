Escalating kinetic pressure on Strait of Hormuz shipping lanes, a Chinese submarine-launched ballistic missile test on Jul 6 assessed by analysts as a strategic signal rather than a routine drill, and NATO unveiling tens of billions in arms deals at the Ankara summit on Jul 7 reveal a single structural shift: the global defence industrial base is being stress-tested simultaneously across three theatres, forcing governments to accelerate procurement timelines and diversify supply chains at a pace that existing capacity cannot absorb. The tension is this -- NATO allies are announcing record arms commitments while several prominent members still fail to meet even baseline spending thresholds, meaning the gap between declared ambition and deliverable capability is widening precisely when deterrence demands credibility. Note that the ATACMS co-production framework is no longer pending: Lockheed Martin and Rheinmetall signed a memorandum of understanding at the Ankara Defence Industry Forum on Jul 7 to jointly produce ATACMS at Rheinmetalls Unterluss site in Germany, though a final joint venture still requires US government approval of the technology transfer.

US Bombs Sirik Qeshm Bandar Abbas Over Hormuz
US Central Command launched strikes on Sirik, Qeshm, and Bandar Abbas on Jul 7, targeting Iranian air defence systems, radar sites, anti-ship missile sites, and coastal surveillance infrastructure, in response to Iranian attacks on three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz (Reuters, CENTCOM, Jul 7). The strikes were described by an Axios source as four to five times larger in scope than the previous round of US strikes roughly ten days earlier. The action also followed the US Treasury revoking the oil-sales waiver granted under the Jun 18 Memorandum of Understanding, which Iran condemned as a clear violation of that agreement. The context of a promised pause around the Khamenei funeral period is not supported by live sources; the strikes occurred as Khamenei funeral proceedings were underway in Qom, and CENTCOM cited Iranian ceasefire violations as justification.

BREAKING Asia-Pacific CRITICAL · 92
China Sub ICBM Test Called Strategic Declaration
On Jul 6, the PLA Navy test-launched a submarine-launched ballistic missile -- assessed by the US State Department as an intercontinental-range ballistic missile -- carrying a dummy warhead from a nuclear-powered submarine in the South China Sea to the Pacific Ocean (CSIS, USNI, CNN, Jul 6-7). China described the launch as routine annual training and said relevant nations were notified in advance; the PLA did not identify the missile type, the submarine, or the precise launch location. Analysts at Asia Society and CSIS assessed the test as China publicly demonstrating a submarine-based second-strike capability for the first time in open ocean. The characterisation that the test is being assessed as undermining the nuclear-weapons-free zone in the South Pacific is supported: the missile landed within or near the South Pacific Nuclear Free Zone defined by the Treaty of Rarotonga, drawing protests from New Zealand, Australia, and Japan. Defense News is not the primary source for this assessment; CSIS and The Diplomat are the lead credible sources as of Jul 7.
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
A submarine-launched ICBM demonstration at this geopolitical moment pressures US and allied missile defence planners to accelerate proliferated tracking and intercept investment, directly benefiting RTX, Northrop Grumman, and L3Harris in the near term.

BREAKING Strait of Hormuz / Iran CRITICAL · 95
Hormuz Tanker Attacks Escalate as US Bombs Iran Ports
Iran attacked three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz on Jul 7: the Qatari-flagged LNG tanker Al Rekayyat and the Saudi-flagged supertanker Wedyan were struck first, with a third vessel hit by a drone less than 24 hours later (CENTCOM, CBS News, Wikipedia Hormuz crisis, Jul 7). The UK Maritime Trade Operations centre confirmed the attacks. Iran did not officially claim responsibility. In response, the US Treasury revoked the oil-sales licence granted under the Islamabad Memorandum, and CENTCOM launched strikes on Iranian military sites near the strait. The framing that countries hoped to restore normal trade flow is broadly accurate: shipping transits had been recovering, with 108 vessel crossings recorded Jul 3-5 per Kpler, before the attacks disrupted that trajectory.
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
Continued disruption to Hormuz shipping and direct US strikes on Iranian port infrastructure signal prolonged supply chain risk for Gulf-based defence operators and energy-linked space and logistics investors.
