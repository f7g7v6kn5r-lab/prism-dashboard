BREAKING North America CRITICAL · 85
SpaceX launches Northrop dual-arm servicing robot
SpaceX launched Northrop Grumman's Mission Robotic Vehicle 1 (MRV-1) on July 21, 2026, sending to geostationary transfer orbit a spacecraft equipped with two robotic arms designed to service, repair, and reposition ageing satellites. The mission also carried three Mission Extension Pods (MEPs), each designed to extend a client satellite's life by up to six years by attaching to it as a fuel backpack. Two MEPs are contracted to service Intelsat satellites and one to service an Optus satellite. Servicing operations are expected to begin in 2027 after the MRV climbs to geostationary orbit using its electric propulsion system. The mission used Falcon 9 booster B1069 on its 32nd and final flight, expended due to the performance requirements of the geostationary transfer orbit trajectory. (Sources: Via Satellite, NASASpaceFlight, Spaceflight Now, July 21, 2026)
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
Successful deployment validates commercial satellite life-extension as a viable market, reducing the write-down risk of ageing GEO assets and opening a new revenue category for both Northrop Grumman and future in-space servicing entrants.

Starship Flight 13 aborts during booster ignition
Starship Flight 13 suffered an abort at T-0 on July 16, 2026, when four of Booster 20s 33 Raptor 3 engines failed to ignite, triggering an automated abort that protected the vehicle and pad. SpaceX subsequently replaced two Raptor engines and is now targeting no earlier than July 23, 2026 for a second launch attempt. The flight is planned to deploy 20 first-generation Starlink V3 satellites on a suborbital trajectory; six of those satellites carry cameras to inspect Ship 40s heat shield in flight. Prior to the abort attempt, Booster 20 completed a record-duration static fire, underscoring the proximity of the vehicle to operational readiness before the anomaly. (Sources: NASASpaceFlight, SpaceNews, Space.com, July 16-20, 2026)
◆ PRISM ANALYSIS
