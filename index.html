Simultaneous kinetic escalation in the Strait of Hormuz and accelerating European defence capitalisation reveal a single structural shift -- the global security architecture is fragmenting into regional deterrence blocs, each racing to fund sovereign strike and air-defence capacity outside traditional US supply chains. The tension is this: multiple rounds of US strikes on Iranian military targets -- beginning with over 80 on 7 July, approximately 90 on 8 July, and approximately 140 overnight into 12 July with a further barrage on 12 July evening -- are generating urgent demand precisely for the Patriot and long-range fires systems that Ukraine, Europe, and Gulf states all need simultaneously, yet supply constraints and multi-year production timelines mean the platforms most in demand are least available -- a bottleneck that favours primes like RTX and NOC while leaving new-space names like RKLB and LUNR exposed to risk-off sentiment. The Strait of Hormuz commercial shipping status is now confirmed severely disrupted as of 13 July 2026: Iran declared the waterway closed, AIS data from Kpler recorded only six vessel transits on 12 July versus a pre-war norm of approximately 138 per day, and US CENTCOM and Iran continue to issue conflicting public statements on navigability -- a sustained disruption already repricing global logistics risk, with Brent crude rising approximately 4.3 percent to around $79.31 a barrel on Monday 13 July, and validating the case for persistent ISR and maritime surveillance constellations over the Gulf as a structural demand signal for the sector.

OPPORTUNITY · TODAY
RTX, the sole Patriot prime, sits at the intersection of every major air-defence demand signal active today — Ukraine domestic production licencing, Gulf theatre deployments, and NATO bilateral agreements — making it the highest-conviction name in the current threat environment.
RISK · TODAY
Iranian physical interdiction of commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz is no longer a tail risk to be monitored -- it is an active, ongoing disruption confirmed by Lloyd's List Intelligence and AIS data as of 13 July 2026. No large vessel above 10,000 dwt has transited the US-coordinated southern route with AIS on since 7 July; Kpler recorded only six transits on 12 July against a pre-war daily norm of approximately 138. Iran has formally declared the waterway closed, while CENTCOM maintains it remains open to lawful transit. The resulting ambiguity is itself a systemic shock to energy markets, Gulf basing logistics, and satellite-served maritime traffic, with immediate read-across to defence contractor deployment tempo and commercial EO operator demand.

BREAKING Middle East CRITICAL · 95
US strikes 140 Iranian targets; Hormuz standoff live
The United States military confirmed approximately 140 strikes on Iranian military targets overnight into Sunday 12 July, targeting missile and drone sites, naval capabilities, ammunition storage, communication networks, and coastal surveillance locations, according to CENTCOM. A further barrage began at 2100 GMT Sunday evening. The strikes followed an IRGC attack on a Cyprus-flagged container ship transiting the strait, which the crew abandoned after it caught fire, with one crew member missing. Iranian state media reported strikes on Qeshm Island and Bandar Abbas; a telecommunications worker was killed on Farur Island and one person was killed at a water pumping station in Mahshahr, according to IRNA as of 13 July 2026. Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed; CENTCOM stated it remains open to lawful transit and that traffic is flowing -- a claim contradicted by AIS data showing only six commercial transits on 12 July, per Kpler, against a pre-war norm of approximately 138 per day.
